Dogfood — the platform evaluates its own shipped skills draft

The Intent Eval Platform's job is to grade other people's skills. The fair test of that claim is whether it grades ours — on the same bench, by the same harness, held to the same signature discipline. This scorecard is that test: the 7-layer skill-binary-eval methodology run against Intent Solutions' own published skills. The measurement is done. The verdicts are deliberately not shown here yet — because a row on this board earns its outcome only when it carries a signature, and these do not carry one yet.

Why a dogfood scorecard is a stronger claim than an external one

Anyone can publish a favourable score of a rival. It is harder — and more honest — to publish the score of your own work under a gate you cannot quietly relax. These skills were run through the identical harness that gates external submissions: the same seven binary layers, the same "no composite score" rule, the same refusal to render a result that has not been signed. If the platform's discipline only survives when it is pointed outward, it is theatre. This page exists so the discipline is pointed inward, in public.

The board

Each row is one of our own shipped skills, measured by the platform. The attestation column is the load-bearing one: signed means an Evidence Bundle exists, is sigstore-signed, and is anchored in the Rekor transparency log at a citable index; pending means the measurement exists but the signing pipeline has not yet run against it. Every row below reads pending today. There is deliberately no composite score column and no outcome shown — composing incommensurable findings into one number is the failure mode this platform exists to refuse, and rendering an outcome that has not been signed is the second one.

Why the verdicts are blank

We have the results. The harness produced a differentiated grade for each skill — genuinely differentiated, which is the point of running it: at least one of these rows did not come back clean, and surfacing that gap is exactly what the eval is for. So the reason the Outcome column is grey is not that there is nothing to report. It is that a verdict on this board is only as trustworthy as the signature under it, and these runs were produced locally, without the keyless signing identity that makes a result verifiable by a stranger. Rendering the verdicts now would mean asking you to trust our word — which is the one thing this platform is built to make unnecessary.

This mirrors the discipline on the Evidence Bench, where a row read pending until its signature was real. We do not render attestation we do not have — not for a competitor, and not for ourselves.

What promotes a row to signed

A pending row becomes a signed row — with its verdict and a citable Rekor index — when the same three things line up that promoted the first Evidence Bench row:

  1. the eval runs under a reproducible GitHub Actions workflow identity, not a person's machine;
  2. its Evidence Bundle is keyless-signed through Fulcio (the same public-good infrastructure behind npm provenance and SLSA);
  3. the signature is anchored in the public Rekor transparency log at an index anyone can fetch and verify with cosign, no account and no keys required.

When that pipeline runs against these three measurements, the rows above flip from pending to signed, the outcomes appear, and each links to its transparency-log entry. Until then the board stays honestly blank. A signature, when it lands, attests authenticity, identity, and time — that this workflow measured this skill at this moment and produced exactly this output. It does not, and will not, declare the reserved skill-binary-eval/v1 predicate under labs.*, and it does not claim the verdict is "correct" beyond what the pre-registered criteria state.
